Measurement-based quantum computation

Quantum Computing · June 2026 · after R. Jozsa, quant-ph/0508124

Download PDF ↓ LaTeX source ↓

In the circuit model, computation is unitary evolution and measurement happens only at the very end. Measurement-based quantum computation (MBQC) inverts this: you start from a large fixed entangled state and compute by measuring qubits one at a time, choosing later measurement bases based on earlier outcomes. Measurement — normally irreversible and destructive — becomes the engine of computation. Two models do this: teleportation quantum computation (TQC) and the one-way / cluster-state quantum computer (1WQC). These notes synthesize Jozsa's introduction: how each works, why each is universal, the three ways they are really the same model, and what MBQC says about complexity.

Notation

Pauli operators \(I, X, Z\) with \(iY = ZX\); \(\oplus\) is addition mod 2. The two-qubit gates are

\[ CX\,|i\rangle|j\rangle = |i\rangle|i\oplus j\rangle, \qquad CZ\,|i\rangle|j\rangle = (-1)^{ij}\,|i\rangle|j\rangle, \]

with \(H=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\left(\begin{smallmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{smallmatrix}\right)\) and \(|\pm\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle\pm|1\rangle)\). Unlike \(CX\), \(CZ\) is symmetric in its two inputs. The Bell basis is generated from \(|B_{00}\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|00\rangle+|11\rangle)\) by Paulis on one half,

\[ |B_{cd}\rangle = (Z^c X^d \otimes I)\,|B_{00}\rangle, \]

and a useful maximally entangled state mixing the standard and Hadamard bases is

\[ |H\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\big(|0\rangle|{+}\rangle + |1\rangle|{-}\rangle\big) = CZ\,|{+}\rangle|{+}\rangle. \]

Gate teleportation: the heart of TQC

Standard teleportation sends \(|\alpha\rangle\) from qubit 1 to qubit 3 using a Bell pair on 2–3 and a Bell measurement on 1–2; outcome \(|B_{cd}\rangle\) leaves qubit 3 in \(X^d Z^c|\alpha\rangle\). Gottesman and Chuang's insight: measure in a rotated Bell basis instead. For any one-qubit gate \(U\), define

\[ |B(U)_{cd}\rangle = (U^\dagger \otimes I)\,|B_{cd}\rangle. \]

Measuring 1–2 in this basis teleports and applies the gate at once: qubit 3 is left in \(X^d Z^c U\,|\psi\rangle\). So a gate is realized purely by a choice of measurement.

The clean way to see it: for any maximally entangled \(|\phi\rangle=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt d}\sum_{i}|i\rangle|i\rangle\) in dimension \(d\), projecting \(|\alpha\rangle_1|\phi\rangle_{23}\) onto \(|\phi\rangle_{12}\) yields \(\tfrac1d|\alpha\rangle_3\) (Lemma 1). Replacing \(|\phi\rangle\) by the rotated state

\[ |\phi(U)\rangle = (U^\dagger \otimes I)\,|\phi\rangle \]

gives \(\tfrac1d\,U|\alpha\rangle_3\) (Lemma 2). Reading these projections as outcomes of a measurement means choosing \(d^2\) unitaries \(U_i\) so that \(\{|\phi(U_i)\rangle\}\) is an orthonormal basis — equivalently \(\{U_i\}\) is a unitary operator basis, \(\mathrm{Tr}(U_i U_j^\dagger)=d\,\delta_{ij}\). One may go further and use \(n\ge d^2\) operators forming a POVM,

\[ \sum_{i=1}^{n} k_i\,|\phi(U_i)\rangle\langle\phi(U_i)| = I_d \otimes I_d, \]

so the Bell measurement can even be replaced by a (generally non-projective) POVM. Two-qubit gates such as \(CZ\) come from the same lemmas in dimension \(d=4\) (a 16-dimensional measurement), or, more cleverly, from smaller measurements on a small entangled gadget.

Byproducts and adaptive measurements

The catch: teleporting a gate never gives \(U|\psi\rangle\) exactly, but \(P\,U|\psi\rangle\) with a random Pauli \(P\) fixed by the outcome. Chaining gates \( \dots U_3 U_2 U_1|\psi\rangle\) therefore produces \( \dots P_3 U_3 P_2 U_2 P_1 U_1|\psi\rangle\) — Paulis interleaved everywhere. The fix is to commute the Paulis to the end and absorb the damage by adapting later measurement bases.

For rotations \(R_x(\theta)=e^{-i\theta X}\), \(R_z(\theta)=e^{-i\theta Z}\) the propagation rules are

\[ R_x(\theta)X = X R_x(\theta),\quad R_x(\theta)Z = Z R_x(-\theta),\quad R_z(\theta)X = X R_z(-\theta),\quad R_z(\theta)Z = Z R_z(\theta). \]

Pushing a byproduct \(Z^aX^b\) past the next rotation only flips the sign of its angle (by \((-1)^b\)). So if the previous outcome was \(b\), you simply measure for \(R_z\big((-1)^b\beta\big)\) instead of \(R_z(\beta)\) — an adaptive basis choice that cancels the byproduct and reinstates the intended gate. Iterating, the whole computation yields

\[ \dots X_2^{m_2} Z_2^{n_2} X_1^{m_1} Z_1^{n_1}\, U\,|\psi\rangle, \]

with the exponents \(m_i, n_i\) bit-sums (mod 2) of measurement outcomes. The final readout is a \(Z\)-basis measurement; a leftover \(X^{m}\) just flips the interpretation of that bit (\(k_i \mapsto k_i\oplus m_i\)) and a leftover \(Z^{n}\) does nothing. A striking consequence: these final \(Z\) measurements are never adaptive, so they can be performed first — the output can be measured before the computation that determines its meaning has even run.

Clifford gates parallelize

The Pauli group \(\mathcal P_n\) is generated by tensor products of \(\pm I, \pm iI, X, Z\). A unitary \(C\) is a Clifford operation if it normalizes the Paulis,

\[ C\,\mathcal P_n\, C^\dagger = \mathcal P_n, \]

i.e. Paulis propagate across \(C\) while \(C\) itself stays fixed — no adaptation needed. \(CZ\), \(H\) (\(HX=ZH\), \(HZ=XH\)) and the \(\pi/4\) phase gate are Clifford, and the whole Clifford group on \(n\) qubits is generated by \(Z, H, P_{\pi/4}, CX\). Because no measurement here depends on another, an entire array of Clifford gates can be done in one parallel layer of measurements: the byproduct exponents are computed afterward as bit-sums, a purely classical \(O(\log)\)-depth job.

This sharpens the Knill–Gottesman theorem (Clifford circuits are classically simulable in poly time, even in log depth): MBQC shows the genuinely quantum content of a Clifford array can be compressed into constant quantum depth plus classical post-processing — a structural statement the classical-simulation result does not give.

The one-way quantum computer

The 1WQC starts from a cluster state: lay qubits in \(|{+}\rangle\) on a 2D grid and apply \(CZ\) to every neighbouring pair. All the \(CZ\)s commute, so the resource is built in constant quantum depth regardless of size. Computation then uses only single-qubit measurements in the bases

\[ M_z = \{|0\rangle,|1\rangle\}, \qquad M(\theta) = \{\,|0\rangle \pm e^{i\theta}|1\rangle\,\}, \]

(\(\theta=0\) is an \(X\)-measurement). Outcomes are uniformly random \(0/1\). As in TQC, gates appear only up to Pauli corrections \(X^aZ^b\) (here called bi-product operators), so measurement bases are again adaptive. The name "one-way" reflects that the pristine cluster is irreversibly consumed as the layers of measurement proceed.

A single-qubit \(U=R_x(\zeta)R_z(\eta)R_x(\xi)\) is implemented on a 5-qubit line: place \(|\psi\rangle\), append four \(|{+}\rangle\), entangle, and measure qubits 1–4 adaptively in bases set by the Euler angles. The output qubit 5 holds \(X^{s_2+s_4}Z^{s_1+s_3}U|\psi\rangle\). The atomic step is one \(M(\theta)\) measurement, which realizes the gate

\[ W(\theta) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & e^{i\theta} \\ 1 & -e^{i\theta}\end{pmatrix} = H\,P(\theta),\qquad P(\theta)=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{i\theta}\end{pmatrix}, \]

leaving the next qubit in \(X^{s}W(-\theta)|\psi\rangle\). Since any one-qubit gate factors as \(U=W(0)W(\theta_1)W(\theta_2)W(\theta_3)\), repeating this step along a wire builds any single-qubit unitary; \(\{CZ, W(\theta)\}\) and \(\{CZ, R_x(\theta), R_z(\theta)\}\) are universal sets. Input states can even be eliminated by starting every wire in \(|{+}\rangle\) and prepending a pattern that prepares \(|\psi\rangle\), so the whole resource is a standard cluster state.

Two-qubit gates need the second grid dimension: there is an explicit measurement pattern realizing \(CZ\) on a small 2D block, and \(CZ\) plus single-qubit gates suffice for universality.

Z-measurements delete qubits

Because a general circuit maps onto an irregular region of a big rectangular cluster, unused sites must be removed. Measuring a site \(A\) in the \(Z\) basis (outcome \(k_A\)) deletes it from the graph: it disconnects \(A\) from its neighbours and leaves behind only an extra \(Z^{k_A}\) byproduct on each former neighbour. These deletion measurements are non-adaptive (like the final readout), so they too can be done first, carving the desired graph out of the cluster before any real computation.

Why one-dimensional clusters fail

2D clusters are universal; 1D clusters are not. A 1D cluster state has the ladder structure of a matrix-product state, and any sequence of single-qubit measurements on it — even adaptive ones — can be classically simulated in poly time. The argument sweeps the chain holding only one or two sites' reduced states at a time, applying a measurement projector at measured sites and a partial trace at unmeasured ones, computing each outcome probability

\[ P(k' \mid a',b',\dots) = \frac{P(a',b',\dots,k',\dots)}{P(a',b',\dots)} \]

by passing through the ladder \(n\) times. If a 1D model were universal, quantum computation would collapse to classical poly time — so genuine quantum speedup needs the 2D entanglement structure.

Three bridges between TQC and 1WQC

The models look different — TQC uses multi-qubit Bell measurements on bipartite pairs, 1WQC uses single-qubit measurements on a multipartite cluster — but they are three views of one idea.

  1. Pairing measurements (Aliferis–Leung). Since \(CX|B_{ij}\rangle = |(-1)^j\rangle|i\rangle\) (first ket in the \(\pm\) basis), a Bell measurement factors into an entangling \(CX\) plus single-qubit measurements. Special pairs of consecutive 1WQC measurements fuse into one Bell measurement, recovering TQC.
  2. One-bit teleportation (Childs et al.; Jorrand–Perdrix). Teleportation decomposes into two identical primitive steps "adjoin \(|{+}\rangle\), entangle with \(CZ\), measure one qubit." Using the \(|H\rangle\) resource with Bell basis \(\{(P\otimes I)|H\rangle\}\) — which \(CZ\) maps to \(\{|{\pm}\rangle|{\pm}\rangle\}\) — makes the two steps symmetric. This shared "one-bit teleportation" is the common atom of both models.
  3. Matrix-product / valence-bond states (Verstraete–Cirac). Take a grid of \(|H\rangle\) bonds and at each site project onto the two-dimensional code subspace with
\[ \Pi = |\bar 0\rangle\langle 0\cdots 0| + |\bar 1\rangle\langle 1\cdots 1|. \]

The projected state is exactly the 1WQC cluster state, and a cluster-state \(M(\theta)\) measurement is precisely a full teleportation that has been "cut down" by \(\Pi\): the rotated Bell states

\[ |a{=}0\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|00\rangle + e^{i\theta}|11\rangle),\qquad |a{=}1\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|00\rangle - e^{i\theta}|11\rangle) \]

lie inside the \(\Pi\)-subspace and reproduce the \(M(\theta)=\{|\bar0\rangle\pm e^{i\theta}|\bar1\rangle\}\) measurement. Each 1WQC ingredient is a descendant, under \(\Pi\), of a teleportation on the valence-bond grid.

Complexity and the classical/quantum split

Both MBQC models are polynomial-time equivalent to the circuit model, with only linear overhead in either direction (a circuit measurement is simulated by an ancilla + \(CX\); a 1WQC pattern is simulated by building the cluster and adding a basis-rotation gate per measurement). So they compute the same class of problems.

What changes is structure. MBQC exposes parallelism the circuit model hides: non-adaptive measurements all commute, so Clifford arrays finish in one layer, and any poly-size circuit maps to a poly number of measurement layers. More deeply, MBQC gives a natural way to separate an algorithm into quantum layers (depth-1 bursts of measurement) and classical layers (the adaptive bookkeeping between them) — a separation with no analogue in classical complexity, and a fitting home for the heavy classical post-processing in algorithms like Shor's. This motivates Jozsa's conjecture:

Any polynomial-time quantum algorithm can be implemented with only \(O(\log n)\) quantum layers interspersed with polynomial-time classical computation.

It would mean polynomial-time classical computation needs only a thin sliver of "quantum assistance" to reach the full power of quantum computation. It is open in general but known to hold for Shor's algorithm (Cleve–Watrous).

Takeaways

  • Universal computation is possible with measurements alone, on a fixed entangled resource.
  • Randomness of outcomes is tamed by Pauli propagation and adaptive bases; what survives is a final Pauli that only relabels the answer.
  • Clifford operations need no adaptation, so they parallelize to constant quantum depth.
  • 2D cluster entanglement is essential — 1D is classically simulable.
  • TQC and 1WQC are the same model seen through teleportation, one-bit teleportation, or matrix-product states.
  • MBQC's real payoff is conceptual: a clean quantum/classical separation and new questions about how little "quantum" a quantum algorithm really needs.
